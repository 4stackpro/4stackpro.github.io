Where is Krakatoa and when was the last time it erupted?

The power of Mother Nature was in full effect as Krakatoa erupted (Picture: Ghazali/EPA-EFE/REX/Shutterstock)

Two eruptions from the Krakatoa volcano were recorded Friday night, with reports that lava and plumes of ash were spewed into the air.

The Center for Volcanology and Geological Disaster Mitigation (PVMBG) reported the first eruption lasted one minute and 12 seconds starting at 9:58pm, with the second one recorded around 10:35pm.

PVMBG also confirmed that three other volcanoes in Indonesia erupted on Friday but they are unrelated as each volcano had a different magma chamber.

Where is Krakatoa?

Krakatoa, also known as Krakatau to the Indonesian people, is a small volcanic island in Indonesia.

The island is located in the Sunda Strait between the islands of Java and Sumatra in the Indonesian province of Lampung.

The closest city is Jakarta, with the volcanic island located about 100 miles west of the city.

Krakatau became two islands in the eruption of 1883. (Picture: Getty Images)

When did Krakatoa last erupt?

There was minor activity reported around May 2019, but the last time a significant eruption of Krakatoa took place was on 22 December 2018 and then again a day later.

The eruption caused a powerful tsunami, with waves up to five meters in height hitting landfall. The tsunami was reported to have affected more than 186 miles of coastline in Sumatra and Java.

On 31 December 2018, the tsunami’s death toll was over 420 people – making it the deadliest volcanic eruption of the 21st century, with over 14,000 injured.

The Queen was one of many who sent messages of support to the people affected by the devastating tsunami.

Krakatoa’s most powerful and history-making eruption was back in 1883.

In August 1883, the eruption of the main island of Krakatoa claimed the lives of over 36,000 people, making it one of the deadliest volcanic eruptions in history.

The eruption also affected the climate and caused temperatures to drop all over the world, with the resulting dust of the eruption causing places as far away as New York reporting a hazy, red skyline for days.
