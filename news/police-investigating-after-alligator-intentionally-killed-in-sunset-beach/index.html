Reminder to Houston: Coronavirus will not stop alligator and snake season

The warmer spring and summer months signal the start of alligator and snake season in Texas, and Houston area officials are reminding residents to keep an eye out for the wildlife especially now that more residents are going for walks outside during the novel coronavirus pandemic.

Gators and snakes tend to be spotted more frequently during this time in subdivisions that are located adjacent to their natural habitat, according to the Texas Parks and Wildlife Department (TPWD). Most recently, a gator was spotted in Fulshear’s Cross Creek Ranch neighborhood and a large-sized water moccasin snake was spotted in Katy’s Firethorne subdivision, according to a March 24 Facebook post from the Fort Bend County Sheriff’s Office.

“Since everyone is home and ‘social distance’ is a term 2020 will be remembered for, please remind your kids to keep their social distance from our native wildlife,” officials said in the post. “The alligators and snakes will be out sunning. Leave them alone and they’ll leave you alone.”

With a total of 115 different snake species, Texas is a “cornucopia of snake diversity,” according to the TPWD. Snakes typically mate and resume foraging activities in the spring, and can be found basking on rocks to warm themselves in the early mornings. The TPWD encourages residents to be vigilant of their surroundings when they are outside during the warmer months, especially near warm rocks or rocky terrain and trails. Despite the high number of species, most snakes in Texas are non-venomous and are completely harmless.

Alligators are usually out moving to breed and find new habitat during the spring and summer, which is why many suburban residents spot them during this time. The TPWD urges residents to never approach a gator - even though it is rare for the animals to chase humans, they can run up for 35 miles per hour on land.

“Never make the mistake of thinking that an alligator is slow and lethargic,” the TPWD states on its website. “Alligators are extremely quick and agile and will defend themselves when cornered.”

The TPWD recommends residents who may have spotted an alligator near their home to keep their pets inside a fenced area or inside their homes for a few days until the animal has moved from the area.

For those who want to get up close and personal with a gator, feeding an alligator is illegal, and could result in a Class C misdemeanor that is punishable by a fine of up to $500, according to the TPWD.
