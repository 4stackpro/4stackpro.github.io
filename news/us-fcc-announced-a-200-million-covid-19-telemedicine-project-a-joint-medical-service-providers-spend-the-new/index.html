美 FCC 宣布 2 億美元 COVID-19 遠距醫療計畫，聯合醫療服務業者共度新冠病毒難關

美國聯邦通訊委員會（Federal Communications Commission，FCC）3 月 31 日宣布一系列打算在新冠病毒大流行期間幫助美國的重要措施。FCC 主席 Ajit Pai 概要分享推動 2 億美元「COVID-19 遠距醫療方案」（COVID-19 Telehealth Program）計畫，將為健康照護服務供應商配備提供遠距醫療服務所需的寬頻連接和裝置。FCC 還放鬆原先持續對濫用手機補貼的打擊措施，並表示至少要到 5 月 29 日才會取消參與者註冊。

此 COVID-19 遠距醫療方案將動用紓困案「CARES Act」撥給 FCC 的資金。一旦獲得批准，將為選定的申請人提供全額資金，以協助他們建置合格的遠距醫療服務和裝置。Pai 還提出一項長期「互聯照護先導方案」（Connected Care Pilot Program）計畫，將在 3 年內提供 1 億美元，以協助健康照護供應商提供遠距醫療服務，並以低收入美國人和退伍軍人為重點服務對象。

FCC 也不會取消任何生命線計畫使用者的存取權

FCC 另外表示，傳染病大流行期間，不會中止使用者繼續享用「生命線計畫」（Lifeline Program），此計畫每月為低收入用戶提供寬頻和語音服務折扣。生命線服務供應商通常被要求取消認為不再符合資格者的訂閱，但 FCC 表示定期篩選合格使用者會延到 5 月 29 日之後才重新展開。

Pai 時代的 FCC 因打擊所謂「生命線計畫」濫用而臭名昭彰。公共誠信中心（Center for Public Integrity）去年 11 月的調查發現，FCC 的激進立場給低收入家庭的傷害可能大於幫助。自 2017 年以來，計畫註冊人數減少 230 萬人。哥倫比亞特區 2018 年 3 月至 2019 年 6 月期間，有 49% 生命線用戶失去補貼，密西西比州、懷俄明州和波多黎各特區也出現類似的急劇下降現象。

「我們的首要任務是在美國人最需要寬頻與電話服務時，確保他們能順暢連線。這些積極措施將大幅降低低收入者在 COVID-19 危機期間失去服務的可能風險。」Pai 於新聞稿表示。美國無線電信業者已承諾在 COVID-19 爆發期間暫停取消服務，而 AT&T、Comcast、Sprint 和 T-Mobile 採取其他措施確保訂戶可正常連線。

COVID-19: 2 more deaths, peak expected in mid-May

CLOSE Social distancing matters. Here is how to do it and how it can help curb the COVID-19 pandemic. USA TODAY

Two more people died from the COVID-19 virus — and 53 more people tested positive for the disease — as of Tuesday in Minnesota.

The number of Minnesotans who tested positive for the novel coronavirus reached a total of 629 on Tuesday morning and 12 people have died, according to the Minnesota Department of Health.

Of that group, 56 remain hospitalized, 26 of them in intensive care. Overall, 112 cases in the state required hospitalization.

People with the disease range in age from 4 months to 104 years old, according to Jan Malcolm, commissioner for the Minnesota Department of Health.

While the median age of infected Minnesotans is 46 years old, the median age of those who have died from the virus is 86 years old.

The youngest person to die from the disease in Minnesota was 58 years old; the oldest was 95.

COVID-19 cases in Minnesota as of Tuesday, March 31, broken down by age range. (Photo: Minnesota Department of Health)

Peak hospitalizations expected after mid-May — but that could change

The confirmed cases are “just the tip of the iceberg,” Malcolm emphasized, noting Minnesotans should assume cases are in all communities. She also urged residents to continue social distancing guidelines during the upcoming holidays, as they fall in the same window as when modeling shows cases are likely to accelerate.

Modeling done by the state and University of Minnesota researchers show a slow build up of cases and then a rapid ramp up if mitigation is not taken, Gov. Tim Walz said during a media briefing Tuesday.

The models show peak hospitalizations due to the COVID-19 virus are likely from mid-May to mid-June.

As experts gather more local data — based on Minnesotans’ underlying health conditions and demographics — they will be able to refine the model with new data, Malcolm said.

But Walz said he’d rather err on the side of “sooner than later” and wants the state to be prepared for the state to react to a surge of cases within the next four weeks.

Social distancing measures such as the stay-at-home order is meant to slow the spread of the virus, which will allow more time to set up temporary hospitals across the state.

Temporary hospitals will likely spring up statewide

Officials have assessed seven facilities — and validated five — that could become temporary hospitals for noncritical patients, according to Joe Kelly, director of Homeland Security and Emergency Management in the state.

The state is looking to identify space for 2,750 temporary hospital beds: 1,000 in the metro and 1,750 at sites in greater Minnesota.

The state will not immediately build out the identified spaces but will convert them “where and when we need to,” Kelly said. If additional hospital beds are needed in a region, the temporary facilities should be operational within 48 to 72 hours.

Kelly said he did not have a cost estimate but said 75% of the cost would be reimbursed by the Federal Emergency Management Agency. He also said the state has not yet determined how to staff the temporary centers.

“I hope that we never need any of the alternate care sites we’re working on, much less all of them at the same time,” Kelly said.

He added that he also hopes the current hospital space in the state will be adequate to care for all patients.

“But as Gov. Walz reminds us, hope is not a plan,” he said.

Where is COVID-19 in Minnesota?

The number of confirmed cases in Stearns County has remained at five for several days. As of Tuesday, Benton County had one confirmed case and Sherburne County had eight.

Confirmed cases have been found in 52 of Minnesota’s 87 counties. Counties added Tuesday include Brown, Carlton, Lyon and Traverse.

The virus has been identified in these counties: Anoka, Beltrami, Benton, Big Stone, Blue Earth, Brown, Carlton, Carver, Cass, Chisago, Clay, Clearwater, Cottonwood, Dakota, Dodge, Douglas, Faribault, Fillmore, Goodhue, Hennepin, Isanti, Itasca, Jackson, Kandiyohi, Lac qui Parle, Le Sueur, Lincoln, Lyon, Mahnomen, Martin, Meeker, Mower, Nicollet, Olmsted, Otter Tail, Ramsey, Renville, Rice, Scott, Sherburne, Sibley, St. Louis, Stearns, Steele, Traverse, Wabasha, Waseca, Washington, Watonwan, Wilkin, Winona and Wright.

As of Tuesday, 19,780 people have been tested. Of those, about 60% were completed in external laboratories; the rest were tested in the state’s public health lab.

Nearly 290 people with confirmed tests have recovered and no longer need to be isolated.

In Minnesota, the median age of patients who require hospitalization is 63; the median age for those who do not require hospitalization is 42.

The median age of hospitalized cases in intensive care units is 62; the youngest person to require hospitalization in the ICU was 33.

As of Tuesday, there were more than 801,000 confirmed cases worldwide, including more than 164,000 in the U.S.
