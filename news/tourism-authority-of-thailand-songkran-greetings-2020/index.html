Tourism Authority of Thailand Songkran greetings 2020

On behalf of all of us at the Tourism Authority of Thailand (TAT), we would like to extend our best wishes to the Thai people and everyone around the Kingdom of Thailand, as they celebrate Songkran and welcome in the Thai New Year on 13 April, 2020.

Traditionally most people who work outside their hometown usually travel home to make merit at temples and to politely pour water on the hands of their elders, asking for blessings of prosperity and a healthy life. This has added significance during 2020, as we take care to ensure everyone remains healthy during the ongoing COVID-19 outbreak.

Because of social distancing, we understand that many traditions are on hold this year while the Songkran spirit remains strong in everyone’s heart.

TAT would like to remind all of you to stay safe this holiday season, prepare for the time when travel and tourism returns, and we can all celebrate Songkran in style with our beloved family and friends.

Happy Songkran!

Best wishes,

Mr. YuthasakSupasorn

Governor

Tourism Authority of Thailand

12 April, 2020, at 13.00 Hrs.

武漢肺炎／泰國取消潑水節假期 籲民眾改在家裡禮佛

（中央社記者呂欣憓曼谷12日專電）明天就是佛曆新年宋干節，今年因為武漢肺炎疫情，泰國政府取消假期，呼籲民眾不要公開聚會、不要進行傳統灑水祈福儀式，也不要前往寺廟，改在家裡禮佛。

明天就是俗稱潑水節的佛曆新年宋干節（Songkran），今年由於2019年冠狀病毒（COVID-19，武漢肺炎）疫情，泰國政府取消4月13日到15日的宋干節假期，將等疫情緩和後再另行公布補假日期。

泰國取消4月13日到15日的宋干節（Songkran）連續假期，警方呼籲民眾不要在路邊聚集潑水，不過商場仍然擺出水槍販賣。中央社記者呂欣憓曼谷攝 109年4月12日

泰國人在宋干節時會到寺廟祈福、造訪長輩並在路邊潑水象徵祝福，泰國公共衛生部今年發起「拯救父母」（Save Parents）活動，建議年輕人不要造訪父母或長輩，減少年長者感染的風險，公衛部建議泰國人利用網路向長輩拜年，如果和長輩住在一起的年輕人，則要減少和長輩的肢體碰觸。

儘管政府取消假期，仍然可能會有人聚在一起或是在路邊潑水慶祝新年，泰國警方警告民眾，公開聚會違反緊急命令，違者將處以最高2年有期徒刑和罰款4萬泰銖。

疫情指揮中心發言人塔維辛（Taweesin Visanuyothin）也表示，不管多少人公開聚集在一起，都有可能面臨法律制裁。

文化部發出聲明，呼籲泰國人今年可以跳過宋干節的傳統灑水祈福儀式（Rot Nam Dam Hua），減少感染風險。這樣的灑水祈福儀式是泰國人宋干節時，全家團聚在一起，年輕人準備花環並點上蠟燭，再將水倒到長輩手中，祈求長輩身體健康和長命百歲。

文化部也呼籲民眾避免前往寺廟，改在家裡禮佛。（編輯：王永志）1090412

We shall meet again

While the continuing pandemic forces the postponement of Thailand’s most enduring holiday celebration, let’s take a look back at more joy-filled days

Thailand without Songkran in April is unprecedented. But in a shocking move, the first meeting of the Centre for Covid-19 Situation Administration last month decided that the annual Songkran holidays – April 13-15 – should be postponed to later, undisclosed dates.

The decision was mainly to minimise the movement of people both domestically and internationally and to restate the importance of social distancing which is, in all, to help curb the spread of Covid-19.

Which means the familiar sight of the country’s summer celebration with people donning aloha shirts, holding water guns, declaring water wars and smearing din sor pong on faces will vanish.

In the wake of the disappearing Songkran, we look at our old news and headlines dating back to 1950s to see how the Thai New Year was celebrated in the good old days – a walk down memory lane.

1958: Dusit Zoo is being relocated from its original location in inner Bangkok to a new home in Pathum Thani province. But in 1958, a Songkran parade was still held at Dusit Zoo, or Khao Din, Thailand’s oldest zoo which was always a favourite attraction for the entire family long before shopping malls took over Bangkok.

1959: Dusit Zoo was a centre of Songkran celebrations where neighbourhood residents hosted various activities including religious rites and Miss Songkran beauty contests. Among the highlights were a kite competition at Pramane Ground, or Sanam Luang.

1963: When social distancing was not a policy, people gathered in Samut Prakan province to celebrate Songkran in the Mon style and parades were organised in Chiang Mai. In Bangkok, the National Museum hosted a water-bathing ceremony for Phra Buddha Sihing, an important and still venerated Buddha statue.

1961: A Songkran celebration at Dusit Zoo.

1984: A happy moment when a gang of young Songkran revellers in Bangkok’s Huai Khwang district threw water at each other on the street despite warnings by the authorities concerned it could lead to accidents.

1990: Songkran always means water splashing and beauty contests. Here we had water wars with tourists in Bangkok’s Patpong area and a beauty pageant in the neighbourhood of Wisut Kasat.

2000: Bangkok’s Khao San Road has become popular as Songkran celebration spot during the past few decades where Thai and international tourists flocked the alley full of restaurants and bars. In preparations for His Majesty the King’s coronation in May last year, however, the Bangkok Metropolitan Administration ordered a ban on entertainment shows and events on the street. Yet the celebration was held in the Thai traditional theme at Lumpini Park.

2002: A foreign Miss Songkran was chosen to head the Thai New Year parade on Khao San Road in 2002 in a traditional northern-style procession.

2005: Songkran is a celebration for everyone — young or old, monk or layman.

2013: Despite tight security protocols in the Deep South where a drive-by shooting took place in Pattani, Songkran was still celebrated nationwide.

2014: Modern-day Songkran celebrations usually see a foam party. Here revellers had fun in a Songkran festival held outside the CentralWorld shopping complex. This year, there is nothing.
