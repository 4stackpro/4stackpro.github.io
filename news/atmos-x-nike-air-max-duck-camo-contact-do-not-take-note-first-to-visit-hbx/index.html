Photographer captures remarkable images of eagle trying to eat rubber duck

This is just one more reason why this month has gone to the birds.

A wildlife photographer in Canada captured incredible images of a young eagle attempting to eat a plastic duck before realizing it wasn’t food, British news agency SWNS reports.

Fifty-two-year-old David Howells took the photos of the eagle in St. John’s, Newfoundland and Labrador, Canada, on March 22. Howells was able to get his camera out prior to the eagle swooping down with its talons extended on what it thought was prey.

“At this time of year the eagles become very active,” Howells said. “The lake freezes over during the bitter weather. We’ve actually just got over the biggest amount of snowfall ever. The bald eagle actually has a very high pitched squark so I heard it coming and caught it perfectly as it swooped in.”

However, the young eagle may have mistaken the rubber duck for prey, only to be surprised, Howell explained.

“At first it looked like the immature bald eagle was tending to it as if it were a chick,” he said. “But then it went to take a bite out of it, realized it wasn’t edible, and just threw it away.”

According to the National Eagle Center, bald eagles primarily have a diet consisting of fish, but can also eat a number of other animals, including birds, squirrels, raccoons and rabbits.

Bald eagles are no longer considered endangered in the U.S., according to the U.S. Fish and Wildlife Service. However, both bald and golden eagles “are protected under the Bald and Golden Eagle Protection Act and the Migratory Bird Treaty Act,” the agency notes.

Coronavirus Florida: ‘Disco Duck,’ dancing and dishes in isolation

When was the last time you were able to sing along to the 1970s classic “Disco Duck”?

See, there actually is an upside to this coronavirus mess.

Let’s face it, you can watch only so many hours of the daily “turn-of-the-screw” updates. Sure, pay attention, be informed, just don’t let the television dominate you.

This is going to sound a bit weird from a guy who has driven to an office building for 41 straight years, but there are some benefits to being cooped up in a house.

For instance, I never knew that I left so many cupboard doors open until my wife pointed it out to me. This is good information. I am trying to rid myself of this bad habit. After all, someone could be blindsided by an open cupboard door, or worse, a dish could fall out and crash to the floor.

These days, when I open a cupboard, I get a few steps away when a little voice in my head says, “Close it … Close it now.”

So I do.

And who knew washing dishes would be so much fun. When you have hours to kill, the dishes become a welcome challenge. In our house we are so tough we forgo the dishwasher and wash them like our ancestors did — soap, water, elbow grease.

No stack of dishes is too large. In fact, I’m thinking of organizing them into subgroups like a true professional — cups first, plates next, silverware last.

Now, back to “Disco Duck,” performed in 1976 by a Memphis disc jockey named Rick Dees and His Cast of Idiots (you can look it up). In a Rolling Stone poll from the 1970s, “Duck” was ranked as one of the Top 10 worst songs of the ’70s.

OK, it’s an acquired taste.

Actually, it really doesn’t matter what you listen to. Everyone with a TV has those stations near the back end of the channel options that play music from a variety of genres — big band, ’60s, ’70s, ’80s, ’90s, bluegrass, country, Broadway, movie scores, hip-hop, gospel and metal. Oh, there are plenty more.

I’m currently stuck on the ’70s channel for a couple of reasons. One, I’m 63. When those songs were popular, music was a huge part of my day-to-day routine. Who didn’t have a fantastic car stereo in those days?

So as you plow through each day, consider giving music, any music, a chance for a comeback.

Music brightens the day and can also serve as an unofficial exercise instructor for those of you who have had your weekly classes cut short. You can crank up the tunes and dance as hard and as long as you can take, minus the screaming in your ear.

And why not? People in your house can either join in or get off the dance floor (in our case, that would be the kitchen floor).
